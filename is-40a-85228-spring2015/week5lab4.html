This week, the due date for our assignment is Friday the 13th. I was curious about why this date is considered "bad luck" in Western countries, so I Googled it. The most interesting result I found was the Wikipedia article which featured a photograph of Leonardo da Vinci's mural painting of The Last Supper, a prominent story in the Christian mythology. Friday the 13th itself turned out to not be very interesting. It's a recent idea, not mentioned in writing before the 19th century and not gaining popularity until the 20th century, according to the Wikipedia article linked to above. But its link to the mural painting is interesting.

Did you know that, according to U.S. copyright law (at least, according to the Licensing Discussion on Wikimedia Commons), photographs of artwork in the public domain are also in the public domain? This means that I can use this fantastic art on my web page, as I have done above. Source attribution is recommended by Wikipedia, and mandatory for academic rigour, so I have linked to the page where you can read all about this image on Wikipedia.

Usually, hot-linking is a very frowned-upon and ethically questionable practice whereby a web page will contain an image whose source is not local but contained on another Web server. However, there are exceptions to this rule. One was pointed out to us by Dr. C. in his introductory lecture at the beginning of the semester; he told us that some web sites that host photos provide mechanisms for their customers to embed the image, hosted on the photo-sharing web site, in their own page or another page elsewhere on the web.

I found a second exception tonight: Hot-linking images from Wikipedia is permitted, providing appropriate attribution is given to the source, and with the proviso that it is not generally a good idea. It's not a good idea because Wikipedia is a dynamic web site, edited by many different authors and subject to change at any time. Some of that change may be deliberate malicious vandalism, and so you may end up serving a page on your web site showing an image you most certainly did not intend. Nevertheless, the image above is hot-linked from the Wikipedia site.

Back to the topic at hand. While Friday the 13th is a relatively modern phenomenon, the Wikipedia article tells us that Friday has been an unlucky day to undertake journeys or begin new projects since at least the 14th Century. Wikipedia's source for this claim is Chaucer's Canterbury Tales. Obligingly, Wikipedia offers a link to The Canterbury Tales, which in turn links us to the Wikipedia page on Chaucer himself.
